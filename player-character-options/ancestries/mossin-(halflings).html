Mossin (Halflings)

Traits

Physical Description

Mossin, often referred to as Halflings, are a small humanoid race known for their resilience and resourcefulness. Standing no taller than the average human child, they are roughly 90 cm in height and weigh between 30 to 60 kg. Their builds are stocky, and their long arms are well-suited to their burrowing lifestyle. Mossin skin colors vary widely, complementing the diverse environment of the savanna. Their large, expressive eyes are typically yellow or orange, though green and blue are rare but cherished. Mossin pride themselves on their variety of braided hairstyles, which range in color from black to brown to grey.

Society

Religion

The Mossin practice Suahin, an animistic faith deeply rooted in their connection to the natural world. They believe every element of nature—a rock, a river, a tree—possesses a spirit, or "Sua," which must be revered and respected. This spiritual outlook emphasizes harmony with the environment and guides their daily actions and decisions.

Cuisine

Mossin cuisine is a celebration of their agricultural and foraging skills, characterized by dishes that are both nutritious and flavorful.

Architecture

Mossin villages, referred to as a burrow, consist of large communal areas with homes around it, organised in a circular pattern. They are typically located in the Savanna, where they live in harmony with the local flora and fauna. During the dry season, the burrows keep them safe from wildfires naturally, and during the wet season a combination of the large, round roof that is constructed over the burrows and elaborate channels protect them from floods.

Names

Mossin names typically reflect aspects of nature or personal traits, and they often carry significant meaning within their communities.

History

Primordial Era

In their early prehistoric culture, the Mossin lived in small, nomadic tribes that roamed the vast Savannahs in search of food, and made homes for themselves by burrowing in the earth. These served as checkpoints along their migratory paths. They hunted and gathered using basic tools made from bone, stone and wood, and developed a deep knowledge of the flora and fauna of their environment. They also developed a rich spiritual culture, with a deep reverence for the natural world and the spirits that they believed inhabited it.
Over time, the Mossin began to develop more complex social structures and systems of governance, as well as more advanced technologies and methods of substinence. They began to develop basic agricultural practives, domesticating certain plant and animal species to ensure a more stable food supply. They also becan to settle in more permanent structures they called Burrows, mostly circular villages and towns with large communal areas, with protection both from flooding and the wildifres that are common in the Savannah.
Near the end of prehistory, three large Settlements were founded.
As the Mossin developed more complex societies and ways of life, they began to develop a system of record-keeping to help them manage their increasingly complex communities. At first, this took the form of simple pictograms and symbols, which were etched into clay for less permanent, and bone for more permanent and important events or transactions. Over time, these symbols evolved into more complex systems of writing, allowing the Mossin to record and preserve their history and culture in ever-greater detail.
The emergence of writing was a critical turning point in the history of the Mossin, allowing them to develop more sophisticated political systems and economic networks. It also allowed them to develop more advanced sciences and technologies, as well as a rich and diverse cultural heritage. In the centuries that followed, the Mossin continued to develop and refine their systems of writing and record-keeping, paving the way for the great empires and civilizations that would come to dominate their world in the years to come.

Era of Storms

At the beginning of the Era of Storms, Mossin population in the Savannas was very large, and they expanded to other regions as well, but during the era of storms their population dwindeled dramatically, with many burrows being flooded, out-of-season wildfires spreading and killing crops and livestock and the resulting hunger. The Era of Storms left many fleeing the Savannas, and many burrows lost to time, abandoned, flooded and caved in.

Era of Legends

Following the devastating Era of Storms, which saw many Mossin burrows lost to floods and wildfires, the Mossin population began a slow but determined resurgence. The savannas of Kataris, though scarred by natural calamities, remained their ancestral home. The Mossin rebuilt their burrows with improved designs—larger communal areas and more intricate flood and fire defenses, allowing them to withstand the extreme weather conditions more effectively.
This time marked a significant evolution in Mossin governance. The largest settlements, Samaq and Qindra, united under a joint democratic government, founding the nation of Samaqindra. This alliance marked the birth of the first democracy in the known world, setting a precedent for future political structures and emphasizing the Mossin values of community and collective decision-making.
As the Mossin expanded their trade networks, they interacted more frequently with neighboring human settlements and distant lands, such as the Dwarven city-states of the Iron Chain and the far northern Kanee Kingdoms. These interactions brought new goods and ideas, enriching Mossin culture and economy. The Mossin became renowned for their intricate handicrafts and their agricultural produce, particularly their herbal concoctions and hearty grains.
Halflings today are known throughout the world. Claiming no place as their own outside of Samaqindra and Thejindra, halflings don't usually control settlements and live among humans within towns or larger cities, or, rarely, as hermits.
